ABOUT THE AUTHOR

The House on Mango Street recalls the story of Esperanza,
a 12-year-old Latina growing up in the Hispanic quarter of
Chicago. Structured as a series of vignettes, readers
learn about Esperanza's experiences with social class,
race, sexuality, identity, and gender.

The novel has over 6 million copies since its initial publication in 1984.
It's required reading in schools across the country.

Sandra Cisneros is a Hispanic-American "poet, short story
writer, novelist, essayist, performer, and artist. Her
numerous awards include NEA fellowships in both poetry and
fiction, a MacArthur Fellowship, and other national and international book awards.

CONNIE'S MEXICO CAFE

In the chapter, “Beautiful and Cruel,” Esperanza believes she is
ugly and that her looks will leave her trapped at home. She wants
to be like the women in the movies who are “beautiful and cruel.”
The women who attract men and love, but never give up their power.

Female owned and operated restaurant Connie’s Mexico Cafe stands
as the oldest Mexican restaurant in Wichita. Owner Carmen Rosales
and managers Adele Jordan and Cici Garcia share their journey of running a restaurant in Wichita’s North End as Latina women.
You can visit their restaurant at 2227 N Broadway Street.

ENRIQUE NAVARRO

In the chapter, “Born Bad,” Esperanza shares a poem with her
dying Aunt Lupe. Aunt Lupe tells Esperanza to never stop writing.
because “it will keep you free.”

Wichita State University Spanish professor Enrique Navarro shares
his own experience with representation (and love for comic books).
He also gives insight into his new book Mexican Americans of
Wichita’s North End (Images of America) and the importance of minority representation in literature and the media. You can
purchase the book on Amazon or Watermark Books & Cafe.

ROCIO DEL ÁGUILA

In the chapter, “Mango Says Goodbye Sometimes,” Esperanza shares how she likes to tell stories to herself, and how she is going to talk about a story where a girl didn’t belong. She'll one day take her
books and say farewell to Mango Street, but she knows that even as
she leaves, she will come back for those who aren't strong enough
to escape on their own, the ones “left behind.”

Wichita State University Spanish Professor Rocío del Águila talks about the importance of sharing your story and paving a path for underrepresented groups to share theirs. She also shares her love
of food and incorporates it through teaching, history, literature,
and media.

ARMANDO MINJÁREZ

In the introduction, “A House of My Own,” Esperanza shares her experiences that led up to her writing and publishing The House On Mango Street. She tells about meeting with other authors, and they
all had the collective thought that art should serve their
communities.

Artist and community organizer Armando Minjárez talks about
his own journey as an immigrant, student, artist, and activist. Additionally, he shares the importance of social justice and equity through art and community organizing.

JESSICA HERNANDEZ

In the chapter, “Bums in the Attic,” Esperanza wishes to have a nice house because she is somewhat ashamed of what she has and doesn’t have. She craves her own house and lets people stay in them
because she’ll never forget where she came from.

Friends University student Jessica Hernandez is a senior majoring
in Graphic Design and Spanish. She shares her experience in an immigrant household and how she strives to use art to help not
only her family but her community.

FRIENDS UNIVERSITY HALO

In the chapter, “The Three Sisters,” three aunts tell Esperanza to
never forget who she is. She’ll always be Esperanza. She’ll always
be Mango Street.

Friends University students Jessica Hernandez, Sebastian Carbajal, Monica Aguilar, and sponsor Teresa Molina speak about their
academic and community engagement through their own Hispanic American Leadership Organization (HALO).

IVÁN GARCIA MIJARES

In the chapter, “Four Skinny Trees,” Esperanza describes the four skinny trees outside her home. When she feels like giving up,
seeing the trees comfort her, as they have grown. Despite the
concrete, they keep reaching upward no matter what.

Wichita State University Spanish Graduate Assistant Iván Garcia Mijares talks about his experiences teaching and growing up in a Mexican-American immigrant household. Additionally, he explains
how his culture prepares and encourages him to teach others about Spanish and Hispanic traditions.

TERESA MOLINA

In the chapter, “Alicia and I Talking on Edna’s Steps,” Esperanza
tells Alicia she will never return to Mango Street until somebody makes it better. However, Alicia says, “Like it or not, you are
Mango Street,” reminding her to never forget where she came from.

Friends University Spanish professor and HALO sponsor Teresa
Molina shares her experience as a Hispanic woman and single
mother. She explores the ideas surrounding conservative
Hispanic traditions, immigration, and community engagement.

KEVIN LOZANO

In the chapter, “Hairs,” Esperanza talks about the different hair
types her family has.

Barber Kevin explains his own experience in cutting hair and
opening up his own barber shop called Ambitions Barber Shop.
He shares how his upbringing motivates him to make a better life
for his family and other Hispanic Americans in Wichita.
You can visit his shop Ambitions Barbershop at 3082 W 13th St N.
